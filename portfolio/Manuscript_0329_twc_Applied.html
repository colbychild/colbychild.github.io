<span style="font-size:12pt;">Neighborhood disparities in air pollution exposure at the US-Mexico border: The intersection of race/ethnicity and older age</span>

<span style="font-size:12pt;">Abstract:</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

Environmental justice (EJ) research has documented greater air pollution risks based on Hispanic/Latinx vs. non-Hispanic White racial/ethnic status. However, EJ research has not focused on the intersection of racial/ethnic and older age statuses in shaping unequal exposures. This is important because minority and older age groups are particularly vulnerable to health effects of air pollution. 

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU1]</a>

</span>

Additionally, EJ research has focused on measures of chronic rather than acute air pollution. We address those limitations by examining intersectional effects of racial/ethnic and older age statuses on chronic and acute exposures to fine particular matter (PM2.5) air pollution in US metropolitan area census tracts within 100km of the US-Mexico border. We use American Community Survey (2012-2016) data to construct sociodemographic variables and USEPA Dowscaler data (2012-2016) to construct chronic and acute measures of PM2.5 exposure. Using multivariable generalized estimating equations, we test for differences in PM2.5 exposures between census tracts with higher vs. lower proportions of older Hispanic residents and older non-Hispanic White residents. We find that as the proportion of the Hispanic population &gt;64 years of age increases, chronic and acute measures of PM2.5 significantly increase. In contrast, as the proportion of the White population &gt;64 years of age increases, changes in chronic and acute measures of PM2.5 are statistically insignificant. Findings illuminate the intersection of racial/ethnic and older age statuses in shaping air pollution inequalities and may inform efforts to mitigate the impacts of air pollution exposures for older Hispanic/Latinx people along the US-Mexico border.

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">Introduction</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

Air pollution has damaging effects on human health (WHO, 2016). An estimated 

<span style="color:black;">

7 million premature deaths annually 

<a>(&gt;13% of total premature deaths) </a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU2]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">worldwide are attributable to air pollution</span>

. 

<span style="color:black;">Of the air pollutants known to affect human health, fine particulate matter 2.5 microns or less in diameter (PM2.5) is the most robust predictor of mortality (Hoek et al., 2013) with </span>

&gt;4 million premature deaths each year attributed 

<span style="color:black;">

to it (Cohen et al., 2017). Not only is air pollution exceptionally harmful, but the environmental justice (EJ) literature has shown that it is distributed inequitably across social groups, with marked exposure inequities in the United States (US) occurring based on racial/ethnic minority and low socioeconomic statuses (Brulle et al., 2006; Mohai et al., 2009; Nadybal et al., 2020)

<a>. </a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC3]</a>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">

Our study addresses two limitations of the EJ literature. First, while numerous EJ studies have focused on the effects of racial/ethnic minority status on exposures to environmental hazards, limited EJ research has examined how racial/ethnic status intersects or interacts with other dimensions of social inequality in shaping disparate exposures. Another limitation of the large body of EJ research on air pollution is that past studies have focused almost exclusively on measures of chronic air pollution (e.g., mean annual pollutant concentrations or health risks attributable to annual air toxics emissions). Our study addresses those limitations by examining intersectional effects of neighborhood-level racial/ethnic and older age statuses on both chronic and acute measures of exposure to PM2.5 air pollution in US metropolitan areas along the US-Mexico border, contexts with well-documented PM2.5 pollution problems (

<a>Colmer et al., 2020</a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC4]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">; Kelly et al., 2010; Lauer et al., 2009; Ordieres et al., 2005; Quintana et al., 2014).</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">

Based on a prior study conducted in El Paso, Texas (Collins et al. 2011), we hypothesized that the increasing proportion of older (i.e., 65 years of age and older) Hispanic/Latinx residents in neighborhoods is associated with greater exposure to PM2.5. We expected this due to Hispanic aging in place within central cities, which prior EJ studies have found to exhibit relatively high levels of air pollution (Collins et al. 2011; 

<a>Jones et al., 2014</a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC5]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">

; 

<a>Pulido 2000</a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC6]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">). In contrast, due to the suburbanization of the older White population, we hypothesized that the increasing proportion of older (i.e., &gt; 64 years of age) non-Hispanic White residents in neighborhoods is associated with less exposure to PM2.5. This study advances knowledge of an under-examined aspect of EJ, specifically the intersection of race/ethnicity and older age in air pollution exposure.  </span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<a>Literature Review</a>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC7]</a>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">

The US-Mexico border represents an appropriate region to examine intersectional inequalities in air pollution exposure. 

<a>Metropolitan areas on the US side of the border are composed of large Hispanic/Latinx and non-Hispanic White populations, which exhibit segregation across residential settings such as central city barrios, suburban developments, and peri-urban colonias. </a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU8]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">

Prior EJ research has documented higher 

<a>air pollution risks </a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU9]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">

based on Hispanic/Latinx vs. non-Hispanic White racial/ethnic status on the US side of the border with Mexico (Collins et al. 2011; Grineski and Collins 2010, 2017). However, EJ research in the US-Mexico border region and elsewhere has not focused on the intersection of racial/ethnic and older age statuses, despite the fact that older adults are particularly 

<a>vulnerable</a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU10]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">

 to air pollution exposures (Pope et al. 2004; Weuve et al. 2012; Power et al. 2011)

<a>. </a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU11]</a>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">

As stated previously, EJ studies of air pollution have almost exclusively focused on chronic exposures. Outside of a recent study by Mullen et al. (2020), no EJ studies have examined inequities in acute air pollution exposures (e.g., based on the number of days wherein PM2.5 concentrations exceed 35 micrograms/m3). This is despite the fact that short-term increases in PM2.5 concentrations have well-documented impacts on 

<a>human health </a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU12]</a>

</span>

<span style="color:black;">

(Braun et al. 2019; Chen et al. 2018; Fajersztajn et al. 2017)

<a>.</a>

</span>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU13]</a>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">An emerging body of EJ research examines how race/ethnicity interacts with other dimensions of social inequality in shaping air pollution exposures </span>

(Chakraborty et al. 2017; Grineski et al. 2013, 2017; Livanos 2015; Waldron 2018). 

<span style="color:black;">This research has focused primarily on the intersection of race/ethnicity with US nativity (i.e., US vs. foreign birth), ancestry, English-language usage/proficiency, and socioeconomic status. As of yet though, only one study has examined the intersection of racial/ethnic status and older age in air pollution exposure (Collins et al. 2011). This study found that Hispanic neighborhoods in El Paso, TX with a higher (vs. lower) proportion of residents &gt;64 years of age had higher cancer risks from air pollution. In contrast, neighborhoods with a higher (vs. lower) proportion of older non-Hispanic Whites had less cancer risk from air pollution. This finding emerged from a bivariate analysis in one US-Mexico border city.  Our study is novel in its focus on the larger US-Mexico border region, and in its more comprehensive analysis of how racial/ethnic status and older age intersect in shaping disparate exposures to PM2.5 air pollution.</span>

</span>

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">Data &amp; Methods</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">Study Area</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

We demarcated the study area as US census tracts within 100 km of the US-Mexico border.    Tracts were only included in our analysis if they had a total population of 499 or greater, at least 99 residents over the age of 64, and located in a metropolitan area core context (i.e., rural-urban commuting area (RUCA) primary code 1 (USDA, 2019)). We also excluded tracts with missing data for analysis variables. We focused on tracts in 

<span style="color:black;">US metropolitan areas along the US-Mexico border, because those contexts have well-documented PM2.5 pollution problems (Kelly et al. 2010; Lauer et al. 2009; Ordieres et al. 2005; Quintana et al. 2014).  After applying those criteria, 294 census tracts were excluded, leaving 1208 in our analyses (no tracts were excluded from the univariate maps, n=1502). </span>

We used census tracts as our analysis unit because they represent the finest resolution for which reliable sociodemographic data (independent variables) are available from the US Census and because they are the unit for which daily PM2.5 estimates are available from the 

<span style="color:black;">US Environmental Protection Agency (</span>

EPA) Downscaler (dependent variables).

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">Dependent Variables</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">We developed three measures of PM2.5 to represent three dependent variables in our analysis.  These measures are all derived from the USEPA Downscaler, which provides mean daily average concentration of PM2.5 in g/m3 for each census tract in the United States (USEPA, 2019). </span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<a>

<span style="color:black;">Downscaler PM2.5 concentration estimates are generated using a Bayesian space-time downscaling fusion model developed by Berrocal et al. (2012) that integrates data from a gridded atmospheric model (i.e., Community Multi-Scale Air Quality Model or CMAQ (Byun et al. 2006)) with point air pollution measurements from the National Air Monitoring Stations/State and Local Air Monitoring Stations (NAMS/SLAMS).  CMAQ estimates gridded averages with no missing values, but is subject to calibration error. That error is accounted for through fusion modeling with monitoring data from NAMS/SLAMS that provide direct, accurate measurements of both air pollutants. Downscaler daily PM2.5 concentration estimates are available at 2010 census tract centroid locations (USEPA 2019) and have increasingly been employed in air pollution epidemiology studies (e.g., Bravo et al. 2017; Son et al. 2020). Berrocal et al. (2012) provide details on the Downscaler modeling approach, validation, and performance. Archived Downscaler data with descriptive files are available from the USEPA (2019).</span>

</a>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[MOU14]</a>

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC15]</a>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">We accessed Downscaler data for the years 2012-2016 to match the time frame of the ACS 5-year estimates used for our independent variables. PM2.5 concentrations have significant variability from year to year, justifying our use of a 5-year period (Koumoutsaris et al. 2008; ODell et al. 2019). For all three measures, we downloaded daily PM2.5 concentration estimates for all US census tracts from 2012-2016 and bound the data to the tracts of our study area described above. </span>

Next, we used those data to calculate chronic and acute PM2.5 exposure measures for the 2012-2016 study period based on policy-relevant thresholds established by the World Health Organization (WHO) and the US Environmental Protection Agencys (USEPA) National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) (USEPA 2012; WHO 2006). We calculated three census tract-level PM2.5 measures, one of chronic exposure and two gauging the frequency of acute short-term (24-hr) exposures. We used the (i) 2012-2016 mean daily average concentration of PM2.5 in g/m3 as a measure of chronic exposure. Our acute exposure measures include counts of the number of days from 2012-2016 for which the tract-level 24-h estimated PM2.5 concentration equaled or exceeded (ii) 25 g/m3 (WHO 24-h mean standard) and (iii) 35 g/m3(EPA 24-h mean NAAQS).

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">Independent Variables</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">We downloaded Hispanic ethnicity by race composition data from the American Community Survey for the years 2012-2016, which we used to calculate mutually exclusive race/ethnicity variables, for the proportions of the tract population that was Hispanic/Latinx as well as non-Hispanic White, Black, American Indian, Asian, Pacific Islander or Other/Multi-race. While each of the non-Hispanic groups excludes the Hispanic/Latinx population, we do not refer to those groups as non-Hispanic in what follows. </span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">To examine tract-level exposure disparities based on older Hispanic/Latinx vs. older White composition, we used data on age by sex by race to create two variables that gauge the proportions of each tracts Hispanic/Latinx population and White population that were over 64 years of age. </span>

We focus on the intersection of Hispanic/Latinx vs. White race/ethnicity with older age in our analysis for two reasons. First, the tract-level populations of Hispanics/Latinxs and Whites are the only ones among racial/ethnic groups that are sufficiently large to do so across the US-Mexico border study area. Second, because these are the only two populations for which age composition is distinguished by the ACS based on Hispanic/Latinx ethnicity and race (i.e., ACS data for age by sex do not distinguish (non)Hispanic/Latinx ethnicity for non-White races).

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

<span style="color:black;">Median household income and the proportion of renter-occupied housing units were included to represent socioeconomic status (SES). We also included median household income squared because of the potential for a non-linear relationship with air pollution exposure (Collins et al. 2017b). Proportion renter-occupancy was included because it is representative of accumulated wealth and stability. A population density variable (total tract population divided by the area of the tract (sq. miles)) was also included in models. Finally, to further control for the urban contextual effects, our models adjust for tract clustering based on the county of location and the age of housing stock.</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">Analysis Approach      </span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">

We undertook our analysis in four steps. First, we mapped the dependent variable and key independent variables and calculated descriptive statistics for all analysis variables. Second, we calculated population-weighted mean chronic and acute PM2.5 exposures for the total populations and our focal populations separately within each of the four US border states (CA, AZ, NM, TX). Our focal populations include Hispanics/Latinxs, Whites, Hispanic/Latinxs 

<span style="color:black;">over 64 years of age</span>

, and Whites 

<span style="color:black;">over 64 years of age</span>

. Population weighted means enable us to describe unadjusted exposures for specific groups. We calculated these by: (a) multiplying the population of each group in each census tract by the PM2.5 value for that tract; (b) summing the group-specific * PM2.5 values for all census tracts; (c) Calculate a total group-specific population value by summing the census tract populations of each group; and (d) dividing the values calculated in steps (a) and (b) by the values calculated in step (c). We did this separately for tracts in each border state (CA, AZ, NM, and TX). 

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">Third, we performed a multivariable analysis using generalized estimating equations (GEEs). GEEs are useful for EJ research because they accommodate non-normally distributed variables and adjust for clustering (Collins et al. 2017a, 2017b; Grineski et al. 2017a, 2017b). We ran 9 separate GEE models, maintaining consistent parameters.  We tried all possible combinations of probability distributions and link functions to find the combination yielding the best model fit. Gamma was used for the Probability Distribution and Log for the Link Function.  The 9 models consist of three subsets of three models.  Each subset includes a separate model for each of the three dependent PM2.5 variables. The first subset of three models includes race/ethnicity, SES, and population density variables as predictors of each dependent variable.  The second subset of three models substitutes the proportion Hispanic 65 and older variable for the proportion Hispanic and includes a new variable proportion White. The proportion Hispanic 65 and over variable is interpretable in reference to the proportion of the Hispanic/Latinx population that is under 65 years of age, adjusting for the other variables in the model and clustering. The third subset of three models substitutes the proportion White 65 and older variable for the proportion Hispanic 65 and older variable and the proportion Hispanic variable for the proportion White variable. The proportion White 65 and older variable in those 3 models is interpretable in reference to the proportion of the White population that is under 65 years of age.</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<span style="font-size:12pt;">Fourth, to clarify the effects of key independent variables on chronic and acute PM2.5 exposures in the GEEs, we calculated estimated marginal (EM) means. EM means provide value estimates of the dependent variables based on specified values for independent variables of interest, adjusting for all other GEE model specifications. Specifically, we calculated EM means at every 10th percentile (from 0 to 100) for each race/ethnicity and race/ethnicity by older age independent variable that was associated with each of the PM2.5 dependent variables. Only EM means with significance were left in our analysis. These were Total Population 65+, Hispanic, Black, and Asian.  </span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<br>

</div>

<span style="color:black;">The three dependent variables mapped in Figure 1 show similar general patterns, although </span>

<span style="color:black;">(i.e., the number of days with PM2.5 &gt; 25 and 35 </span>

<span style="color:black;"> For all three variables, the highest values are in the tracts of the largest metropolitan areas along the border (San Diego, Imperial Valley, El Paso, and Brownsville) and the lowest values are in tracts beyond. This holds true across the dependent variables except for the count of days with a PM2.5 concentration over 35 </span>

g/m3, for which tracts in Brownsville have zero values. 

<span style="color:black;">Within the large metropolitan areas, values are highest in urban centers and tend to decrease moving outward towards tracts with less population density. g/m3

<span style="color:black;">).</span>

<span style="font-size:12pt;">We mapped four independent variables in Figure 2. Unlike the dependent variables with higher values in metropolitan areas, these independent variables each show more complex and unique patterns across the border region. The highest proportion Hispanic tracts were concentrated most heavily in Texas. Tracts in New Mexico, Arizona, and California had lower proportion Hispanic except for small clusters in San Diego, Imperial Valley, and Tucson. This pattern changes for the proportion Hispanic 65 and older, with higher proportion tracts seen through California, Arizona, and New Mexico. For the proportion White 65 and older, the pattern appears more random, with pockets of high proportion tracts spread across the border region.</span>

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<a>Discussion &amp; Conclusions</a>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC18]</a>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="font-size:medium;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">

<a>Important limitations of this study are that (a) aggregated (as opposed to individual) data was used, (b) micro-level exposure was not accounted for, (c) health impacts for higher air pollution exposure in specific populations along the border are not known, (d) indoor exposure or workplace and commute exposure were not included. Future research should address these limitations.</a>

<span style="font-size:8pt;">

<a style="color:#954f72;">[TC19]</a>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="font-size:medium;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">Our first research question was how does the intersection of racial/ethnic status and age shape unequal exposure to air pollution? Specifically, how does older age (65+) vs younger age (Below 65) interact with racial/ethnic status in shaping unequal exposure to air pollution? In order to interpret our results in relation to this question, its important to make the distinction between statistical significance and public health significance. Small increases in PM2.5 at lower levels of exposure have real health outcomes and smaller changes at lower levels of exposure have steeper changes in health outcomes (Burnett 2018; USEPA 2013; Pope et al. 2009). This means that a higher exposure to PM2.5 for older Hispanic vs younger Hispanic can exacerbate negative health outcomes since older adults are already more vulnerable to the impacts of PM2.5 exposure. Our findings reveal that Hispanic status and older age status are independently associated with increased exposures to chronic and acute PM2.5 across census tracts of the border region. This indicates that older age interacts with Hispanic status to increase exposures to chronic and acute PM2.5. In contrast, we found no such interaction between older age and White status. Since older age is generally associated with increased exposure, our results suggest that while Hispanic status amplifies risks of PM2.5 exposures in older age, Whiteness protects against such risks. EM Means graphed in Figure 3 show how increases in proportion Hispanic 65 and over remain relatively stable until around the 90th percentile when exposure spikes. This information helps contextualize the GEE result by revealing that census tracts with a very high proportion of older Hispanic individuals saw dramatic increases in PM2.5 exposure. This pattern holds across chronic and acute exposure to PM2.5.  </span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="font-size:medium;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">Our second research question was what differences exist in exposure patterns between chronic and acute measures of PM2.5? The acute measure of PM2.5 showed stronger contrasts between the high values of urban tracts and the low values of rural tracts. PM2.5 mean exposure showed a steadier gradient from city centers to more rural areas.  The acute measure of days in which PM2.5 was over 25 g/m3 had stronger spikes than Mean PM2.5 in certain urban areas showing that this acute measure is important for understanding where dangerous short-term spikes in PM2.5 occur that are not captured by the Mean PM2.5 measure. </span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</span>

</div>

<div style="text-align:left;">

<span style="font-family:fbft; font-weight:400;">

<span style="font-size:medium;">

<span style="color:#000000;">

<span style="font-style:normal;">

<span style="text-transform:none;">There is a clear pattern of environmental injustice across the chronic and acute measures of PM2.5 across the border region. However, there are areas of particular concern where high levels of PM2.5 intersect with high concentrations of older Hispanic residents. For example, Figure 1() reveals high acute exposure to PM2.5 in central El Paso (TX) tracts as well as higher proportions of older Hispanic populations in the same context. Practical interventions should focus on neighborhoods with relatively high PM2.5 exposures and high composition of older Hispanic people.  El Paso, in the context of the previous point, could seek out ways to reduce transportation emissions at major border crossings. Future research should seek to pinpoint other specific areas where interventions to lower air pollution exposures for can happen. </span>

Introduction

Air pollution has damaging effects on human health (WHO, 2016). An estimated 7 million premature deaths annually (>13% of the total premature deaths) [MOU4] worldwide are attributable to air pollution. Of the air pollutants known to affect human health, fine particulate matter 2.5 microns or less in diameter (PM2.5) is the most robust predictor of mortality (Hoek et al., 2013) with >4 million premature deaths each year attributed to it (Cohen et al., 2017). Not only is air pollution exceptionally harmful, but the environmental justice (EJ) literature has shown that it is distributed inequitably across social groups, with marked exposure inequities in the United States (US) occurring based on racial/ethnic minority and low socioeconomic statuses (Brulle et al., 2006; Mohai et al., 2009; Nadybal et al., 2020). [TC5] 

Our study addresses two limitations of the EJ literature. First, while numerous EJ studies have focused on the effects of racial/ethnic minority status on exposures to environmental hazards, limited EJ research has examined how racial/ethnic status intersects or interacts with other dimensions of social inequality in shaping disparate exposures. Another limitation of the large body of EJ research on air pollution is that past studies have focused almost exclusively on measures of chronic air pollution (e.g., mean annual pollutant concentrations or health risks attributable to annual air toxics emissions). This Our study addresses those limitations by examining intersectional effects of neighborhood-level racial/ethnic and older age statuses on both chronic and acute measures of exposure to PM2.5 air pollution in US metropolitan areas along the US-Mexico border, contexts with well-documented PM2.5 pollution problems (Colmer et al., 2020[TC6] ; Kelly et al., 2010; Lauer et al., 2009; Ordieres et al., 2005; Quintana et al., 2014).

Based on a prior study conducted in El Paso, Texas (Collins et al. 2011), we hypothesized that the increasing proportion of older (i.e., > 654 years of age and older) Hispanic/Latinx residents in neighborhoods is associated with greater exposure to PM2.5. We expected this due to Hispanic aging in place within central citiesy barrios, which prior EJ studies have found to exhibit have relatively high levels of PM2.5[MOU7] air pollution (Collins et al. 2011; Jones et al., 2014[TC8] ; Pulido 2000[TC9] ). In contrast, due to the suburbanization of the older White population, , including their concentration in retirement communities on metropolitan area fringes, we hypothesized that the increasing proportion of older (i.e., > 64 years of age) non-Hispanic White residents in neighborhoods is associated with less exposure to PM2.5. This study advances knowledge of an under-examined aspect of EJ, specifically the intersection of race/ethnicity and older age in air pollution exposures.

Literature Review[TC10] 

The US-Mexico border represents an appropriate region to examine intersectional inequalities in air pollution exposure. Metropolitan areas on the US side of the border are composed of large Hispanic/Latinx and non-Hispanic White populations, which exhibit segregation across residential settings such as central city barrios, suburban developments, and peri-urban colonias. [MOU11] Prior EJ research has documented higher air pollution risks [MOU12] based on Hispanic/Latinx vs. non-Hispanic White racial/ethnic status on the US side of the border with Mexico (Collins et al. 2011; Grineski and Collins 2010, 2017). However, EJ research in the US-Mexico border region and elsewhere has not focused on the intersection of racial/ethnic and older age statuses, despite the fact that older adults are particularly vulnerable[MOU13]  to air pollution exposures (Pope et al. 2004; Weuve et al. 2012; Power et al. 2011). [MOU14] 

As stated previously, EJ studies of air pollution have almost exclusively focused on chronic exposures. Outside of a recent study by Mullen et al. (2020), no EJ studies have examined inequities in acute air pollution exposures (e.g., based on the number of days wherein PM2.5 concentrations exceed 35 micrograms/m3). This is despite the fact that short-term increases in PM2.5 concentrations have well-documented impacts on human health [MOU15] (Braun et al. 2019; Chen et al. 2018; Fajersztajn et al. 2017).[MOU16] 

An emerging body of EJ research examines how race/ethnicity interacts with other dimensions of social inequality in shaping air pollution exposures (Chakraborty et al. 2017; Grineski et al. 2013, 2017; Livanos 2015; Waldron 2018). This research has focused primarily on the intersection of race/ethnicity with US nativity (i.e., US vs. foreign birth), ancestry, English-language usage/proficiency, and socioeconomic status. As of yet though, only one study has examined the intersection of racial/ethnic status and older age in air pollution exposure (Collins et al. 2011). This study found that Hispanic neighborhoods in El Paso, TX with a higher (vs. lower) proportion of residents >64 years of age had higher cancer risks from air pollution. In contrast, neighborhoods with a higher (vs. lower) proportion of older non-Hispanic Whites had less cancer risk from air pollution. This finding emerged from a bivariate analysis in one US-Mexico border city. Our study is novel in its focus on the larger US-Mexico border region, and in its more comprehensive analysis of how racial/ethnic status and older age intersect in shaping disparate exposures to PM2.5 air pollution.

 

Data & Methods

Study Area

We demarcated the study area as US census tracts within 100 km of the US-Mexico border. Tracts were only included in our analysis if they had a total population of 499 or greater, at least 99 residents over the age of 64, and located in a metropolitan area core context (i.e., rural-urban commuting area (RUCA) primary code 1 (USDA, 2019)). We also excluded tracts with missing data for analysis variables. We focused on tracts in US metropolitan areas along the US-Mexico border, because those contexts have well-documented PM2.5 pollution problems (Kelly et al. 2010; Lauer et al. 2009; Ordieres et al. 2005; Quintana et al. 2014). After applying those criteria, 294 census tracts were excluded, leaving 1208 in our analyses (no tracts were excluded from the univariate maps, n=1502). We used census tracts as our analysis unit because they represent the finest resolution for which reliable sociodemographic data (independent variables) are available from the US Census and because they are the unit for which daily PM2.5 estimates are available from the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) Downscaler (dependent variables).

Dependent Variables

We developed three measures of PM2.5 to represent three dependent variables in our analysis. These measures are all derived from the USEPA Downscaler, which provides mean daily average concentration of PM2.5 in g/m3 for each census tract in the United States (USEPA, 2019).

Downscaler PM2.5 concentration estimates are generated using a Bayesian space-time downscaling fusion model developed by Berrocal et al. (2012) that integrates data from a gridded atmospheric model (i.e., Community Multi-Scale Air Quality Model or CMAQ (Byun et al. 2006)) with point air pollution measurements from the National Air Monitoring Stations/State and Local Air Monitoring Stations (NAMS/SLAMS). CMAQ estimates gridded averages with no missing values, but is subject to calibration error. That error is accounted for through fusion modeling with monitoring data from NAMS/SLAMS that provide direct, accurate measurements of both air pollutants. Downscaler daily PM2.5 concentration estimates are available at 2010 census tract centroid locations (USEPA 2019) and have increasingly been employed in air pollution epidemiology studies (e.g., Bravo et al. 2017; Son et al. 2020). Berrocal et al. (2012) provide details on the Downscaler modeling approach, validation, and performance. Archived Downscaler data with descriptive files are available from the USEPA (2019).[MOU17] [TC18] 

We accessed Downscaler data for the years 2012-2016 to match the time frame of the ACS 5-year estimates used for our independent variables. PM2.5 concentrations have significant variability from year to year, justifying our use of a 5-year period (Koumoutsaris et al. 2008; ODell et al. 2019). For all three measures, we downloaded daily PM2.5 concentration estimates for all US census tracts from 2012-2016 and bound the data to the tracts of our study area described above. Next, we used those data to calculate chronic and acute PM2.5 exposure measures for the 2012-2016 study period based on policy-relevant thresholds established by the World Health Organization (WHO) and the US Environmental Protection Agencys (USEPA) National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) (USEPA 2012; WHO 2006). We calculated three census tract-level PM2.5 measures, one of chronic exposure and two gauging the frequency of acute short-term (24-hr) exposures. We used the (i) 2012-2016 mean daily average concentration of PM2.5 in g/m3 as a measure of chronic exposure. Our acute exposure measures include counts of the number of days from 2012-2016 for which the tract-level 24-h estimated PM2.5 concentration equaled or exceeded (ii) 25 g/m3 (WHO 24-h mean standard) and (iii) 35 g/m3 (EPA 24-h mean NAAQS).

Independent Variables

We downloaded Hispanic ethnicity by race composition data from the American Community Survey for the years 2012-2016, which we used to calculate mutually exclusive race/ethnicity variables, for the proportions of the tract population that was Hispanic/Latinx as well as non-Hispanic White, Black, American Indian, Asian, Pacific Islander or Other/Multi-race. While each of the non-Hispanic groups excludes the Hispanic/Latinx population, we do not refer to those groups as non-Hispanic in what follows.

To examine tract-level exposure disparities based on older Hispanic/Latinx vs. older White composition, we used data on age by sex by race to create two variables that gauge the proportions of each tracts Hispanic/Latinx population and White population that were over 64 years of age. We focus on the intersection of Hispanic/Latinx vs. White race/ethnicity with older age in our analysis for two reasons. First, the tract-level populations of Hispanics/Latinxs and Whites are the only ones among racial/ethnic groups that are sufficiently large to do so across the US-Mexico border study area. Second, because these are the only two populations for which age composition is distinguished by the ACS based on Hispanic/Latinx ethnicity and race (i.e., ACS data for age by sex do not distinguish (non)Hispanic/Latinx ethnicity for non-White races).

Median household income and the proportion of renter-occupied housing units were included to represent socioeconomic status (SES). We also included median household income squared because of the potential for a non-linear relationship with air pollution exposure (Collins et al. 2017b). Proportion renter-occupancy was included because it is representative of accumulated wealth and stability. A population density variable (total tract population divided by the area of the tract (sq. miles)) was also included in models. Finally, to further control for the urban contextual effects, our models adjust for tract clustering based on the county of location and the age of housing stock.

Analysis Approach

We undertook our analysis in four steps. First, we mapped the dependent variable and key independent variables and calculated descriptive statistics for all analysis variables. Second, we calculated population-weighted mean chronic and acute PM2.5 exposures for the total populations and our focal populations separately within each of the four US border states (CA, AZ, NM, TX). Our focal populations include Hispanics/Latinxs, Whites, Hispanic/Latinxs over 64 years of age, and Whites over 64 years of age. Population weighted means enable us to describe unadjusted exposures for specific groups. We calculated these by: (a) multiplying the population of each group in each census tract by the PM2.5 value for that tract; (b) summing the group-specific * PM2.5 values for all census tracts; (c) Calculate a total group-specific population value by summing the census tract populations of each group; and (d) dividing the values calculated in steps (a) and (b) by the values calculated in step (c). We did this separately for tracts in each border state (CA, AZ, NM, and TX).

Third, we performed a multivariable analysis using generalized estimating equations (GEEs). GEEs are useful for EJ research because they accommodate non-normally distributed variables and adjust for clustering (Collins et al. 2017a, 2017b; Grineski et al. 2017a, 2017b). We ran 9 separate GEE models, maintaining consistent parameters. We tried all possible combinations of probability distributions and link functions to find the combination yielding the best model fit. Gamma was used for the Probability Distribution and Log for the Link Function. The 9 models consist of three subsets of three models. Each subset includes a separate model for each of the three dependent PM2.5 variables. The first subset of three models includes race/ethnicity, SES, and population density variables as predictors of each dependent variable. The second subset of three models substitutes the proportion Hispanic 65 and older variable for the proportion Hispanic and includes a new variable proportion White. The proportion Hispanic 65 and over variable is interpretable in reference to the proportion of the Hispanic/Latinx population that is under 65 years of age, adjusting for the other variables in the model and clustering. The third subset of three models substitutes the proportion White 65 and older variable for the proportion Hispanic 65 and older variable and the proportion Hispanic variable for the proportion White variable. The proportion White 65 and older variable in those 3 models is interpretable in reference to the proportion of the White population that is under 65 years of age.

Fourth, to clarify the effects of key independent variables on chronic and acute PM2.5 exposures in the GEEs, we calculated estimated marginal (EM) means. EM means provide value estimates of the dependent variables based on specified values for independent variables of interest, adjusting for all other GEE model specifications. Specifically, we calculated EM means at every 10th percentile (from 0 to 100) for each race/ethnicity and race/ethnicity by older age independent variable that was associated with each of the PM2.5 dependent variables. Only EM means with significance were left in our analysis. These were Total Population 65+, Hispanic, Black, and Asian.

Univariate Results

The three dependent variables mapped in Figure 1 show similar general patterns, although the chronic pollution variable (i.e., mean PM2.5) displays more gradual spatial variation while the two acute pollution variables (i.e., the number of days with PM2.5 > 25 and 35 g/m3) have more concentrated high values. For all three variables, the highest values are in the tracts of the largest metropolitan areas along the border (San Diego, Imperial Valley, El Paso, and Brownsville) and the lowest values are in tracts beyond. This holds true across the dependent variables except for the count of days with a PM2.5 concentration over 35 g/m3, for which tracts in Brownsville have zero values. Within the large metropolitan areas, values are highest in urban centers and tend to decrease moving outward towards tracts with less population density. While San Diego tracts have the highest chronic pollution (i.e., mean PM2.5) values, El Paso tracts exhibit the greatest number of days with PM2.5 over the two acute thresholds (i.e., 25 and 35 g/m3). Although Californias Imperial Valley is not a large population center, tracts there exhibit high PM2.5 across all three variables, which is partly attributable to the nearby Mexican metropolis of Mexicali.

This pattern changes for the proportion Hispanic 65 and older, with higher proportion tracts seen through California, Arizona, and New Mexico. Multivariable Results

Table 3 includes results from the 9 GEE models. For the base model variables, we found that increases in the proportions of the population that were Hispanic, Black, Asian, and age 65+, as well as housing units that were Renter Occupied, were associated with significant increases in PM2.5 values across the dependent variables. This was with the exceptions of the proportion 65+ for Mean PM2.5 and the proportion Black for # days PM2.5 > 35 g/m3, both of which exhibited positive and statistically non-significant associations.

Exponentiated values of Beta (Table 3) provide percentage increases or decreases in the dependent variable range based on the independent variable to assist with relative and comparative interpretation of GEE results. As the proportion Hispanic increased, Mean PM2.5 increased by 1.5% (B=0.015; 95% CI=0.008 to 0.022[MOU19] [TC20] ), the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 increased by 23.4% (0.210; 0.123 to 0.297), and the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 35 g/m3 increased by 38.9% (0.329; 0.200 to 0.457). As the proportion Black increased, Mean PM2.5 increased by 0.6% (0.006; 0.003 to 0.008), and the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 increased by 5.5% (0.053; 0.020 to 0.087). As the proportion Asian increased, Mean PM2.5 increased by 0.7% (0.007; 0.006 to 0.009), the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 increased by 7.3% (0.070; 0.052 to 0.088), and the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 35 g/m3 increase by 12.7% (0.120; 0.080 to 0.159). As the proportion of the total population 65+ increased, the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 increased by 0.2% (0.057; 0.019 to 0.094), and the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 35 g/m3 increased by 5.8% (0.076; 0.031 to 0.120). As the proportion renter occupied increased, Mean PM2.5 increased by 0.8% (0.008; 0.004 to 0.012), the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 increased by 21.8% (0.197; 0.141 to 0.253), and the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 35 g/m3 increased by 24.7% (0.221; 0.162 to 0.280). An isolated finding was that as the proportion Pacific Islander increased, the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 increased by 1.3% (0.013; 0.002 to 0.024).

Additionally, we found a significant and positive association between median household income and each of the PM2.5 measures, such that higher income was associated with increased exposure. The effect was nonlinear in the case of the # days PM2.5 > 35 g/m3, such that there was a slight decline in that acute exposure measure at the highest levels of median household income. Adjusting for other variables in the GEEs, the population density control had relatively weak positive associations with the PM2.5 measures.

Continuing with Table 3 in the minority subgroup variables, we found that a standard deviation increase in the proportion of Hispanic 65 and older is associated with a 0.1% increase in Mean PM2.5 (0.001; 0.000 to 0.002), a 5.9% increase in the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 (0.058; 0.032 to 0.084), and a 5.1% increase in the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 35 g/m3 (0.050; 0.016 to 0.083). The results for Hispanic 65 and older are in reference to the proportion of the Hispanic population age 64 and less. We also found an inverse relationship between proportion white and PM2.5 values. That is, as the proportion White increases, Mean PM2.5 exposure decreases by 1.1% (-0.011; -0.017 to -0.006), the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 25 g/m3 decreases by 86.1% (-0.150; -0.22 to -0.080), and the number of days in which PM2.5 was greater than 35 g/m3 decreases by 78.6% (-0.241; -0.341 to -0.142). We found that the association between the proportion White 65 and older and increases PM2.5 exposure was not significant. The White 65 and older variable is in reference to the proportion fo the White population age 64 and less.

Discussion & Conclusions[TC21] 

Important limitations of this study are that (a) aggregated (as opposed to individual) data was used, (b) micro-level exposure was not accounted for, (c) health impacts for higher air pollution exposure in specific populations along the border are not known, (d) indoor exposure or workplace and commute exposure were not included. Future research should address these limitations.[TC22] 

Our first research question was how does the intersection of racial/ethnic status and age shape unequal exposure to air pollution? Specifically, how does older age (65+) vs younger age (Below 65) interact with racial/ethnic status in shaping unequal exposure to air pollution? In order to interpret our results in relation to this question, its important to make the distinction between statistical significance and public health significance. Small increases in PM2.5 at lower levels of exposure have real health outcomes and smaller changes at lower levels of exposure have steeper changes in health outcomes (Burnett 2018; USEPA 2013; Pope et al. 2009). This means that a higher exposure to PM2.5 for older Hispanic vs younger Hispanic can exacerbate negative health outcomes since older adults are already more vulnerable to the impacts of PM2.5 exposure. Our findings reveal that Hispanic status and older age status are independently associated with increased exposures to chronic and acute PM2.5 across census tracts of the border region. This indicates that older age interacts with Hispanic status to increase exposures to chronic and acute PM2.5. In contrast, we found no such interaction between older age and White status. Since older age is generally associated with increased exposure, our results suggest that while Hispanic status amplifies risks of PM2.5 exposures in older age, Whiteness protects against such risks. EM Means graphed in Figure 3 show how increases in proportion Hispanic 65 and over remain relatively stable until around the 90th percentile when exposure spikes. This information helps contextualize the GEE result by revealing that census tracts with a very high proportion of older Hispanic individuals saw dramatic increases in PM2.5 exposure. This pattern holds across chronic and acute exposure to PM2.5.

Our second research question was what differences exist in exposure patterns between chronic and acute measures of PM2.5? The acute measure of PM2.5 showed stronger contrasts between the high values of urban tracts and the low values of rural tracts. PM2.5 mean exposure showed a steadier gradient from city centers to more rural areas. The acute measure of days in which PM2.5 was over 25 g/m3 had stronger spikes than Mean PM2.5 in certain urban areas showing that this acute measure is important for understanding where dangerous short-term spikes in PM2.5 occur that are not captured by the Mean PM2.5 measure.

There is a clear pattern of environmental injustice across the chronic and acute measures of PM2.5 across the border region. However, there are areas of particular concern where high levels of PM2.5 intersect with high concentrations of older Hispanic residents. For example, Figure 1() reveals high acute exposure to PM2.5 in central El Paso (TX) tracts as well as higher proportions of older Hispanic populations in the same context. Practical interventions should focus on neighborhoods with relatively high PM2.5 exposures and high composition of older Hispanic people. El Paso, in the context of the previous point, could seek out ways to reduce transportation emissions at major border crossings. Future research should seek to pinpoint other specific areas where interventions to lower air pollution exposures for can happen.
